Step 6: Understand Correlation and Covariance

In this stage you learn two related ideas: covariance and correlation, which both describe how two variables move together.

Covariance tells you the direction of the relationship, and correlation gives you both the direction and strength on a simple scale from ‚àí1 to +1.


1Ô∏è‚É£ Intuition: move together or opposite?

Think of two variables like study hours and marks, or rainfall and crop yield.

  • If both tend to increase together, the relationship is positive.
  • If one increases while the other tends to decrease, the relationship is negative.

Covariance captures this ‚Äúsame direction or opposite‚Äù movement, but its raw value is hard to interpret, so correlation is usually preferred.

2Ô∏è‚É£ What is covariance (simple idea)?

Covariance measures how two variables vary together around their means; a positive value means they move in the same direction on average, a negative value means opposite direction.

  • Positive covariance ‚Üí when study hours are above average, marks are often above average too.
  • Negative covariance ‚Üí when price is above average, demand tends to be below average.

A drawback is that covariance is not standardized; its value depends on units and can be any large positive or negative number.

3Ô∏è‚É£ What is correlation (simple idea)?

Correlation is a standardized form of covariance that always lies between ‚àí1 and +1, which makes relationships easy to compare.

  • +1 ‚Üí perfect positive linear relationship.
  • 0 ‚Üí no linear relationship.
  • ‚àí1 ‚Üí perfect negative linear relationship.

Many guides treat values around 0.8 or ‚àí0.8 as strong, around 0.4‚Äì0.6 as moderate, and near 0 as weak or no linear relationship.

4Ô∏è‚É£ Using Pandas: corr() and cov()

Pandas can compute covariance and correlation matrices for your numeric columns with one line of code. 

import pandas as pd

df = pd.read_csv("students_clean.csv")

print(df[["study_hours", "marks"]].head())

# Correlation matrix
print("Correlation:")
print(df[["study_hours", "marks"]].corr())

# Covariance matrix
print("Covariance:")
print(df[["study_hours", "marks"]].cov())

The diagonal values show each variable‚Äôs covariance or correlation with itself, while the off‚Äëdiagonal entries show the relationship between pairs.

5Ô∏è‚É£ Visualizing correlation with a heatmap

A correlation matrix heatmap lets you see relationships between many variables at once using colors. 

import seaborn as sns
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

corr_matrix = df.corr(numeric_only=True)

plt.figure(figsize=(6, 5))
sns.heatmap(corr_matrix, annot=True, cmap="coolwarm", fmt=".2f")
plt.title("Correlation Heatmap")
plt.tight_layout()
plt.show()

Strong positive correlations appear as warmer colors, strong negative as cooler colors, and near‚Äëzero correlations as neutral shades.
