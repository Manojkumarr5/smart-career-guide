Step 2: Descriptive and Inferential Statistics

In this stage you learn two big ideas in statistics: descriptive statistics (to summarize data you already have) and inferential statistics (to make guesses about a bigger group using a small sample).

These ideas help you describe marks, sales, or rainfall data clearly and later decide whether changes you see are real or just random noise.


1Ô∏è‚É£ Descriptive statistics ‚Äì describing data

Descriptive statistics are numbers and charts that summarize a dataset, such as mean, median, mode, range, variance, and standard deviation.

  • Mean: the average value (sum of values √∑ number of values).
  • Median: the middle value when data is sorted.
  • Mode: the value that appears most often.
  • Range: highest value minus lowest value.
  • Variance / Standard Deviation: how spread out the values are around the mean.

Example dataset: marks of 5 students in maths:

marks = [60, 75, 80, 85, 90]

For this small list, you can compute mean, median, and mode by hand or with a calculator; later you will use Python or Excel for larger datasets.

2Ô∏è‚É£ Inferential statistics ‚Äì guessing about a population

Inferential statistics use a sample (small group) to say something about a population (big group), using probability.

  • Example: survey 50 students in your college and use that data to estimate the average marks of all students in the college.
  • Common tools include confidence intervals and hypothesis tests that help decide if a difference is likely real or due to chance.

At this stage, remember: descriptive = ‚Äúdescribe what you see‚Äù, inferential = ‚Äúuse what you see to guess about a bigger group‚Äù.

4Ô∏è‚É£ Basic idea of hypothesis testing (very simple)

A hypothesis test is a structured way to decide whether an observed difference is likely real or just random variation.

  • Null hypothesis (H‚ÇÄ): states there is no effect or no difference (for example, ‚ÄúSection A and Section B have the same average marks‚Äù).[web:349]
  • Alternative hypothesis (H‚ÇÅ): states there is some effect or difference (for example, ‚ÄúSection A has higher average marks than Section B‚Äù).[web:349][web:352]

For now, do not worry about test formulas; just know that hypothesis testing helps you say ‚Äúthis change is probably real‚Äù or ‚Äúthis might be random‚Äù.

5Ô∏è‚É£ Descriptive vs inferential ‚Äì quick comparison

Both branches are used together in real data projects: you first describe your sample and then try to generalize to a population.

  • Descriptive: summarize the data you collected (tables, charts, averages, measures of spread).
  • Inferential: use sample data plus probability to draw conclusions about a larger population.
